Dangers of Mountain Climbing

The chances of dying in mountain climbing is 1 in 15,700 annually, which is about a thousand times more dangerous than snowboarding.


Dangers of High Altitude Climbing

Unsurprisingly, the odds are worst in high altitude climbing. This guide does not discourage anyone from mountaineering or rock climbing expeditions, but rather, how to prepare for one.

Navigation Tool

In the recent decades, personal phone or portable GPS devices hit the world by storm, allowing anyone with a device to track their position, speed, and elevation at any given moment. For long trips, dedicated GPS should be prefered over smartphone as they are prone to singal lost. It’s also important to note that neither smart phone or GPS can replace a strong grasp of navigational techniques.

Whether for circumnavigation or mountaineering, the compass has been used for hundred of years for navigation. The red end of magnetic needle generally points in direction of local magnetic field, which in most cases, is not the same as true north. The animation on the right should give you a good indication of how magnetic fields have changed over time. The good news is that the compass can be easily re-orientated and most have adjustor for offseting declination.

Paired with a compass, topographic map can be great, especially for those whose looking for adventures. A location is determined by pinpointing the bearings from multiple locations on map, a process known as triangulation. However, to order to successfully triangulate, known features or landmarks must exist to use as a point of reference. (Learn More)

Illumination and Heating

Whether it is setting up camp, starting an early climb, or navigating at night, a reliable source of light is of vital importance. Headlamps use LEDs exclusively as their light source, not only light but also energy-efficient and long-lasting. Although not as portable, fire is also a great source of light and can be used to melt snow. In alpine regions, where tinder and wood are scarce, multifuel stoves are used instead. They run on gas canisters, white gas, kerosene and denatured alcohol. It is also a good idea to bring a fire starter kit in case of equipment malfunction.

First-aid supplies

As the body adjusting higher altitude, it's normal for acclimatization to occur. It's important watch out for signs when climbing and adjust pace if needed. Symptoms includes headache, irregular breathing, and sleeplessness. Mountaineering is an extreme sport and with a lot of casualty, having an first aid kit could mean the difference between life and death. To prepare for more traumatic situations, I’d bring a few more items to being around a wounded person more bearable, such as painkiller. For treating common injuries and sickness, here are the things you might need:

  1. Aspirin (elevation sickness)
  2. Adhesive bandages
  3. Medical tape/Duct tape
  4. Tweezers, small scissors, and safety pins
  5. Disinfecting ointment/Neosporin

More Climbing Gears

Knife and Multi-tool

Most mountaineers carry some sort of multi tool, such as swiss army knife and certainly many carry a multi-tool. Cutting tools are useful when cords or clothing get stuck or caught. If there are unsafe rope left-behind from old expeditions, the best thing to do is get rid of it with a knife or multi-tool before anyone gets hurt.

Nutrition

For short climbs, carbohydrates such as bananas, clip bar, fruit dried fruit, bananas, quick oats, and rice can effectively increase blood glucose level. For more intense climbs such as alpine climbing, include low GI grains such as brown rice and quinoa for longer sustained energy.

Hydration

At high altitude, the body uses more energy than it would at sea level to perform. As a result, dehydration and fatigue are common amount climbers. The good news is consuming a balanced amount of water, sodium, and electrolytes can prevent the body from altitude induced fatigue.

Emergency shelter

    While we all hope for carefree, happy-go-lucky days in the mountain, things don’t always go according to plan. It’s important to have a few emergency items in hand. They can keep you warm while waiting for rescue or an avalanche to pass.

  1. Tent
  2. Sleep Bag
  3. Tarp
  4. Reflective Blanket
